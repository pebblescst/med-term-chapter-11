Functions of the Respiratory System
Gas exchange
O₂ into blood and CO₂ out at alveoli (external respiration)
Internal respiration
O₂ / CO₂ exchange between blood and body cells
Acid-base balance
CO₂ regulation controls blood pH (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃)
Voice production
Larynx (laryng/o) and vocal cords produce sound (phon/o)
Olfaction
Sense of smell via olfactory receptors in nasal cavity
Protection
Cilia, mucus, and cough reflex trap and remove pathogens
Specialty: Pulmonology · Specialist: Pulmonologist (pulmon/o) · Also: Thoracic surgery (thorac/o) · Key combining forms: pneum/o, pneumon/o (lung/air), pulmon/o (lung), bronch/o (bronchus), pleur/o (pleura)
Respiratory Tract — Upper vs. Lower
Upper respiratory tract
Nose · Paranasal sinuses · Pharynx (nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx) · Larynx · Epiglottis · Trachea (upper portion)
Lower respiratory tract
Trachea (lower) · Bronchi · Bronchioles · Alveoli · Pleura · Diaphragm
Mediastinum — central compartment of thoracic cavity between the lungs; contains heart, trachea, esophagus, great vessels. Pleura (pleur/o) — two-layered serous membrane surrounding each lung; visceral (on lung) and parietal (on chest wall).

The Respiratory Tract — Air Pathway
1
Nose & Nasal Cavitynas/o, rhin/o
Air enters here; nasal hairs and mucus filter particles; nasal conchae warm and humidify air; olfactory receptors detect smell (olfaction). Nasal septum (sept/o) divides left and right. Paranasal sinuses (sin/o, sinus/o) drain into nasal cavity.
2
Pharynxpharyng/o
Throat; shared passage for air and food. Three regions: Nasopharynx (behind nose; contains pharyngeal tonsils/adenoids [adenoid/o] and eustachian tube openings), Oropharynx (behind mouth; palatine tonsils [tonsill/o]), Laryngopharynx (lowest; leads to larynx and esophagus).
3
Larynx (Voice Box)laryng/o
Contains vocal cords for sound production (phon/o). Epiglottis = flap that covers larynx during swallowing to prevent aspiration. Adam's apple = thyroid cartilage.
4
Trachea (Windpipe)trache/o
~12 cm rigid tube reinforced by C-shaped cartilage rings; carries air to bronchi. Cilia sweep mucus upward (mucociliary escalator). Carina = point where trachea bifurcates into left and right main bronchi.
5
Bronchi & Bronchiolesbronch/o, bronchiol/o
Right main bronchus: shorter, wider, more vertical → more likely to aspirate foreign objects. Left main bronchus: longer, narrower. Bronchi → secondary (lobar) → tertiary (segmental) → bronchioles → terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles. No cartilage in bronchioles — smooth muscle only.
6
Alveolialveol/o
Tiny air sacs at the end of respiratory bronchioles; ~300 million per lung; walls are ONE cell thick; surrounded by pulmonary capillaries. Site of gas exchange (O₂ in, CO₂ out). Covered by surfactant (reduces surface tension; prevents collapse).
Pleura & Lung Lobes
Pleura (pleur/o) — double-layered serous membrane
Visceral pleura — adheres directly to lung surface
Parietal pleura — lines chest wall
Pleural space — potential space between layers; contains lubricating fluid
Right lung — 3 lobes (lob/o): upper, middle, lower
Left lung — 2 lobes: upper and lower (cardiac notch for heart)
Lobectomy (lob/o + -ectomy) = surgical removal of a lung lobe; most common surgery for early-stage lung cancer. Pneumonectomy = removal of entire lung.
Diaphragm & Paranasal Sinuses
Diaphragm (diaphragm/o) — dome-shaped muscle separating thoracic and abdominal cavities; primary muscle of inspiration; contracts → flattens → ↑ thoracic volume → air drawn in

Paranasal sinuses (sin/o, sinus/o) — air-filled cavities in skull bones:
• Frontal sinuses (forehead)
• Maxillary sinuses (cheekbones) — largest
• Ethmoid sinuses (between eyes)
• Sphenoid sinuses (behind ethmoid)
Eustachian tube (salping/o) — connects nasopharynx to middle ear; equalizes ear pressure; pathway for middle ear infections from pharynx.
Breathing & Respiration Terms — -pnea suffix
The suffix -pnea = breathing condition. Prefix modifies type: eu- (normal), dys- (difficult), a- (without), brady- (slow), tachy- (fast), hyper- (excessive), ortho- (straight/upright).
Eupnea (eu- + -pnea)
Normal, easy breathing. eu- = good/normal. Rate: 12–20 breaths/min in adults.
Dyspnea (dys- + -pnea)
Difficult or labored breathing; subjective breathlessness; symptom of many cardiopulmonary diseases.
Apnea (a- + -pnea)
Absence of breathing. Sleep apnea: repetitive cessation during sleep → hypoxia → arousals.
Orthopnea (orth/o + -pnea)
Difficulty breathing when lying flat; relieved by sitting upright. orth/o = straight. Classic sign of heart failure.
Bradypnea (brady- + -pnea)
Abnormally SLOW breathing rate. Causes: opioid overdose, elevated ICP, severe hypothyroidism.
Tachypnea (tachy- + -pnea)
Abnormally RAPID breathing rate (>20/min). Causes: fever, anxiety, hypoxia, pulmonary embolism.
Hyperpnea (hyper- + -pnea)
Increased depth AND rate of breathing; as in exercise or metabolic acidosis (Kussmaul breathing).
Hypercapnia (hyper- + capn/o)
Excess CO₂ in blood; from hypoventilation; leads to respiratory acidosis. CO₂ = capn/o.
Inhalation vs. Exhalation
Inhalation / Inspiration (in- prefix)
Active process. Diaphragm contracts + external intercostals contract → thoracic cavity expands → pressure drops → air flows IN.
Exhalation / Expiration (ex- prefix)
Passive at rest. Diaphragm and intercostals relax → thoracic cavity recoils → pressure rises → air flows OUT.
Spirometry (spir/o + -metry) — measures lung volumes and airflow; key test for COPD and asthma; values include FVC (forced vital capacity) and FEV₁.
Sputum & Other Respiratory Signs
Sputum — mucus + cellular debris coughed up from lower airways; culture diagnoses infection
Hemoptysis (hem/o + -ptysis) — coughing up blood; from TB, lung cancer, bronchiectasis
Rhinorrhea (rhin/o + -rrhea) — runny nose; watery nasal discharge
Cyanosis (cyan/o + -osis) — blue skin from O₂ deficiency
Oximetry (ox/o + -metry) — measurement of O₂ saturation; pulse oximetry normal: ≥95%
Thoracodynia (thorac/o + -dynia) — chest pain
Rhinorrhea
Runny nose; watery nasal discharge. rhin/o + -rrhea (flow). Symptom of URI, allergies, sinusitis.
Sinusitis
Inflammation of the paranasal sinuses. sinus/o + -itis. Acute (viral URI) or chronic (bacterial, fungal, polyps). Symptoms: facial pain/pressure, nasal congestion, purulent discharge, anosmia.
Rhinomycosis
Fungal infection of the nose. rhin/o + myc/o (fungus) + -osis. Can be serious in immunocompromised patients (mucormycosis).
Pharyngitis
Inflammation of the pharynx (throat). pharyng/o + -itis. Most commonly viral (>90%); Group A Strep (bacterial) requires antibiotics to prevent rheumatic fever.
Laryngitis
Inflammation of the larynx. laryng/o + -itis. Hoarseness, loss of voice (aphonia). Viral (URI) is most common; voice rest is treatment.
Nasal polyps
Non-cancerous benign growths in nasal passages; associated with chronic sinusitis, allergies, and aspirin sensitivity (Samter's triad). Cause congestion and anosmia.
Pneumonia
Infection/inflammation of lung parenchyma (alveoli). pneumon/o + -ia. Causes: bacterial (Streptococcus pneumoniae most common), viral (influenza, COVID-19), fungal, atypical (Mycoplasma). Symptoms: fever, productive cough, pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea. Diagnosis: chest X-ray (consolidation).
Tuberculosis (TB)
Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection; primarily affects lungs; spread by airborne droplets. Primary TB often latent (PPD/TST positive). Active TB: hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss, fever. Treatment: 6-month RIPE therapy (Rifampin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol).
Bronchitis
Inflammation of the bronchi. bronch/o + -itis. Acute (viral, productive cough 3 weeks): self-limited. Chronic (COPD): productive cough ≥3 months/year for ≥2 years; from smoking.
Atelectasis
Collapse of all or part of a lung or alveoli. atelos (incomplete) + ectasis (dilation/expansion). Causes: mucus plugging, prolonged bed rest, post-surgical. Leads to hypoxia. Prevention: deep breathing, incentive spirometry.
Pneumoconiosis
Occupational lung disease from inhaled dust particles. pneumon/o + coni/o (dust) + -osis. Types: silicosis (silica dust), asbestosis (asbestos), coal workers' (black lung). Leads to fibrosis and progressive restrictive lung disease.
Asthma
Chronic inflammatory airway disease with reversible bronchoconstriction. astr- = panting. Triggers: allergens, exercise, cold air, infections. Symptoms: wheezing, dyspnea, chest tightness, cough. Treatment: bronchodilators (beta-2 agonists), inhaled corticosteroids.
Emphysema
Permanent destruction of alveolar walls → enlarged air spaces → reduced surface area for gas exchange. Part of COPD. Barrel chest, pursed-lip breathing. Caused by smoking and alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
COPD — Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
Umbrella term for chronic bronchitis + emphysema; progressive, irreversible airflow obstruction. Leading cause: cigarette smoking. Diagnosed by spirometry (FEV₁/FVC <0.70). Treatment: bronchodilators, steroids, O₂ therapy, pulmonary rehab.
Bronchiectasis
Permanent, abnormal widening and scarring of bronchi (-ectasis = dilation/expansion); from recurrent infections (CF, TB). Copious purulent sputum, hemoptysis. Treated with airway clearance and antibiotics.
Obstructive vs. Restrictive lung disease: Obstructive (asthma, COPD, bronchiectasis) = airflow out is blocked; FEV₁ reduced more than FVC. Restrictive (pneumoconiosis, pulmonary fibrosis) = lung cannot fully expand; both FEV₁ and FVC reduced equally.
Pleurisy (Pleuritis)
Inflammation of the pleura. pleur/o + -isy (-itis). Sharp pleuritic chest pain that worsens with breathing; friction rub on auscultation. Causes: viral, pneumonia, PE, autoimmune.
Pneumothorax
Air in the pleural space (pneum/o + -thorax). Spontaneous (tall thin males, bleb rupture), traumatic, or tension (life-threatening emergency — tracheal deviation away). Treatment: needle decompression, chest tube.
Hemothorax
Blood in the pleural space (hem/o + -thorax). From trauma, surgery, malignancy. → compresses lung → dyspnea. Treatment: chest tube drainage.
Pulmonary embolism (PE)
Blood clot (usually from DVT) lodges in pulmonary artery; blocks blood flow → hypoxia. Classic triad: dyspnea, chest pain, hemoptysis. Diagnosis: CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA). Treatment: anticoagulation.
Pulmonary fibrosis
Scarring and stiffening of lung tissue; progressive and irreversible restrictive lung disease. Idiopathic (IPF) or from toxins, autoimmune, radiation. Bilateral interstitial infiltrates on imaging. Treated with antifibrotics.
Lung carcinoma
Most lethal cancer in US; majority from smoking. Types: SCLC (small cell) and NSCLC (non-small cell: adenocarcinoma, squamous, large cell). Symptoms: hemoptysis, weight loss, persistent cough, hoarseness. Screening: low-dose CT (LDCT) in high-risk smokers.
Diagnostic Procedures
Spirometry (spir/o + -metry)
Measures lung volumes and airflow; diagnoses and monitors asthma, COPD; measures FVC, FEV₁, FEV₁/FVC ratio
Oximetry (ox/o + -metry)
Pulse oximetry; non-invasive measurement of blood O₂ saturation via fingertip probe; normal SpO₂ ≥95%
Chest X-ray (CXR)
First-line imaging; detects pneumonia (consolidation), pneumothorax, pleural effusion, cardiomegaly, lung masses
CT chest / CTPA
Detailed cross-sectional imaging; diagnoses pulmonary embolism (CTPA), lung cancer, interstitial disease; low-dose CT screens for lung cancer
Bronchoscopy (bronch/o + -scopy)
Flexible scope passed through mouth/nose into bronchi; visualizes airways; biopsy; BAL (bronchoalveolar lavage); removes foreign bodies
Auscultation & percussion (A&P)
Auscultation: listen with stethoscope (breath sounds, crackles, wheezes, friction rub). Percussion: tap chest to detect fluid (dull) or air (hyperresonant).
ABG (arterial blood gas)
Measures pH, PaO₂, PaCO₂, HCO₃⁻; assesses respiratory and metabolic acid-base balance; diagnoses respiratory failure
Sputum culture
Lab culture of expectorated sputum; identifies causative organism in pneumonia, TB, bronchiectasis; guides antibiotic therapy
Therapeutic Procedures
Airway management
Tracheostomy (trache/o + -stomy)
Surgical creation of a permanent or semi-permanent opening into the trachea through the neck; for long-term ventilation, airway obstruction
Tracheotomy (trache/o + -tomy)
Incision into the trachea; emergency airway access; temporary. Tracheotomy = incision; tracheostomy = opening (stoma created)
Endotracheal intubation
Tube inserted through mouth/nose into trachea; secures airway; allows mechanical ventilation; used in general anesthesia and respiratory failure
Surgery
Lobectomy (lob/o + -ectomy)
Surgical removal of a lung lobe; most common surgery for early-stage non-small cell lung cancer
Tonsillectomy (tonsill/o + -ectomy)
Surgical removal of tonsils; treats recurrent tonsillitis, sleep apnea, peritonsillar abscess
Septoplasty (sept/o + -plasty)
Surgical repair of deviated nasal septum; improves nasal airflow and treats chronic obstruction
Pharmacology
Bronchodilators (beta-2 agonists) Inhaled corticosteroids Antibiotics (pneumonia/TB) Antifungals Antihistamines Oxygen therapy Antifibrotics (IPF)
